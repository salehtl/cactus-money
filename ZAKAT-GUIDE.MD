# The Complete Guide to Calculating Zakat: Schools of Thought, Stocks, Gold, and More

Zakat is one of the five pillars of Islam — a mandatory act of worship that purifies wealth and supports those in need. While all Muslims agree on its fundamental obligation, the practical details of *how* to calculate zakat vary across the four major Sunni schools of jurisprudence (madhabs).

This guide will walk you through everything you need to know to calculate your zakat correctly, whether you hold cash, gold, stocks, or other assets.

---

## Part 1: The Four Schools of Thought (Madhabs)

Before diving into calculations, it's essential to understand that the **Hanafi**, **Maliki**, **Shafi'i**, and **Hanbali** schools have meaningful differences in their zakat rulings. Here are the key distinctions:

### Who Must Pay Zakat?

| Issue | Hanafi | Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali |
|-------|--------|--------------------------|
| **Children's wealth** | No zakat due | Zakat is obligatory; guardian must pay |
| **Insane person's wealth** | No zakat due | Zakat is obligatory; guardian must pay |

### Does Debt Reduce Your Zakat Liability?

| Madhab | Ruling |
|--------|--------|
| **Hanafi** | Yes — debts are deducted from your zakatable assets |
| **Shafi'i** | No — zakat is due even if debt covers your entire property |
| **Maliki** | Debts reduce zakat on gold and silver only, not on crops or livestock |
| **Hanbali** | Yes — pay zakat only on the remainder after meeting debts |

### When Must You Pay?

| Madhab | Timing |
|--------|--------|
| **Hanafi & Hanbali** | Not required to pay immediately when due |
| **Shafi'i & Maliki** | Must pay immediately when zakat becomes obligatory |
| **Maliki** | Cannot pay zakat *before* a full lunar year passes |
| **Hanafi, Shafi'i, Hanbali** | Permissible to pay zakat in advance |

## Part 2: The Nisab (Minimum Threshold)

Zakat is only obligatory when your wealth reaches the **nisab** — the minimum threshold. This is measured in gold or silver:

| Madhab | Gold Nisab | Silver Nisab |
|--------|------------|--------------|
| **Hanafi** | 87.48g | 612.36g |
| **Maliki** | 85g | ~595g |
| **Shafi'i** | 84.7g | 592.9g |
| **Hanbali** | 85g | ~595g |

> **Important:** The Hanafi school uses the **silver standard** when calculating nisab for mixed assets, which typically results in a lower threshold and more people being liable for zakat.

**Current Approximate Values (check current prices):**
- Gold nisab (85g): ~$7,000–8,000 USD
- Silver nisab (595g): ~$500–600 USD

## Part 3: Calculating Zakat on Cash

Cash is the simplest asset to calculate zakat on.

### Formula:
$$\text{Zakat} = \text{Total Cash} \times 2.5\%$$

### What Counts as "Cash":
- Physical currency
- Bank account balances (checking, savings)
- Money market accounts
- Deposits and certificates

### Example:
| Asset | Amount |
|-------|--------|
| Checking account | $5,000 |
| Savings account | $15,000 |
| Cash at home | $500 |
| **Total** | **$20,500** |
| **Zakat Due (2.5%)** | **$512.50** |

## Part 4: Calculating Zakat on Gold

### The Big Difference: Personal Jewelry

This is one of the most significant points of disagreement between the schools:

| Madhab | Zakat on Personal Jewelry? |
|--------|---------------------------|
| **Hanafi** | **YES** — zakat is due on ALL gold/silver jewelry |
| **Maliki, Shafi'i, Hanbali** | **NO** — no zakat on jewelry for permissible personal use |

### What's Always Zakatable:
- Gold bars and coins held as investment
- Gold not in regular use
- Gold exceeding normal personal use (excessive jewelry)

### Calculation:
$$\text{Zakat} = \text{Weight of Gold (grams)} \times \text{Current Gold Price} \times 2.5\%$$

### Example:
| Item | Weight | Current Value |
|------|--------|---------------|
| Gold necklace | 25g | $1,875 |
| Gold bracelet | 15g | $1,125 |
| Gold coins (investment) | 50g | $3,750 |
| **Total** | **90g** | **$6,750** |

- **Hanafi ruling:** Zakat due on all = $168.75
- **Other schools:** Zakat due only on investment gold (50g) = $93.75

## Part 5: Calculating Zakat on Stocks

Stock zakat depends on **your intention** for holding the shares.

### Scenario 1: Short-Term Trading (Buying to Sell)

If you actively trade stocks for profit, they are treated as **trade goods**:

$$\text{Zakat} = \text{Market Value of Shares} \times 2.5\%$$

**Example:** You own $50,000 worth of stocks for trading
- **Zakat Due:** $50,000 × 2.5% = **$1,250**

---

### Scenario 2: Long-Term Investment (Holding for Dividends/Growth)

If you hold shares as a long-term investment, you only pay zakat on your **proportionate share of the company's zakatable assets** — not the market value.

#### Step-by-Step Process:

**Step 1:** Find the company's zakatable assets from their annual report:
- Cash and cash equivalents
- Receivables
- Inventory

**Step 2:** Calculate zakatable assets as a percentage of market cap

**Step 3:** Apply that percentage to your shares, then pay 2.5%

#### Real Example: Air Arabia

| Metric | Value |
|--------|-------|
| Company's zakatable assets | AED 6.19 billion |
| Market capitalization | AED 25.06 billion |
| **Zakatable % of market cap** | **24.71%** |

If you own AED 100,000 worth of shares:
- Zakatable portion: AED 100,000 × 24.71% = AED 24,710
- **Zakat due:** AED 24,710 × 2.5% = **AED 617.75**

Compare this to the trading approach: AED 100,000 × 2.5% = AED 2,500 — a significant difference!

---

### The 25% Shortcut Method

If you cannot access a company's financial statements, scholars recommend assuming **25% of your shares' value is zakatable**:

$$\text{Zakat} = \text{Share Value} \times 25\% \times 2.5\%$$

Or simplified: **Share Value × 0.625%**

---

### Special Case: Islamic Banks

Some Islamic banks (like Dubai Islamic Bank) pay a **portion** of zakat on behalf of shareholders. However, this typically covers only the zakat on retained earnings and reserves — **you are still responsible for the remainder**.

Always check the bank's official zakat statement and deduct what they've paid from your total obligation to avoid double-paying.

## Part 6: Zakatable Commodities

### Agricultural Produce

| Madhab | What's Zakatable |
|--------|------------------|
| **Hanafi** | ALL agricultural produce including vegetables |
| **Maliki & Shafi'i** | Only staples that are stored (wheat, barley, rice, dates, raisins) |
| **Hanbali** | Everything weighed and stored from fruits and grains |

**Rates:**
- 10% if rain-irrigated (no cost for water)
- 5% if artificially irrigated

**Nisab:** 5 wasq (~910 kg) for all schools **except Hanafi**, which requires zakat regardless of quantity.

---

### Livestock

Zakat applies to camels, cattle, sheep, and goats with specific nisab amounts.

| Madhab | Key Difference |
|--------|----------------|
| **Maliki** | Zakat on both grazing AND non-grazing livestock, including working animals |
| **Others** | Only grazing livestock not used for work |

---

### Minerals and Honey

| Asset | Hanafi | Maliki | Shafi'i | Hanbali |
|-------|--------|--------|---------|---------|
| **Minerals** | 20% on all | 2.5% on gold/silver only | 2.5% on gold/silver only | 20% on all |
| **Honey** | Zakat required | No zakat | No zakat | Zakat required |

## Part 7: Business Assets

If you own a business, zakat is due only on **liquid/zakatable assets**, NOT on the total business valuation.

### Zakatable (Pay 2.5%):
- Cash in hand and bank
- Inventory/stock for resale (at market value)
- Raw materials intended for sale
- Receivables expected to be collected

### NOT Zakatable:
- Buildings and property used in business
- Machinery and equipment
- Vehicles used in operations
- Goodwill and brand value

### Formula:
$$\text{Zakat} = (\text{Zakatable Assets} - \text{Current Liabilities}^*) \times 2.5\%$$

*\*Hanafi and Hanbali allow deducting liabilities; Shafi'i does not.*

## Final Checklist Before Paying Zakat

1. ✅ Confirm your total wealth exceeds the nisab
2. ✅ Ensure one lunar year (hawl) has passed
3. ✅ Identify which madhab you follow for specific rulings
4. ✅ Calculate each asset category separately
5. ✅ Deduct debts if your madhab permits
6. ✅ Check if any institution has paid zakat on your behalf (e.g., Islamic banks)
7. ✅ Pay to eligible recipients in the eight categories

## Conclusion

Calculating zakat requires attention to detail, but the process becomes straightforward once you understand the principles. The key takeaways are:

- **Know your madhab** — the schools differ on jewelry, debt deductions, and timing
- **Distinguish between trading and investment** — especially for stocks
- **Focus on liquid assets** — not total valuations for businesses and investments
- **Use the 25% shortcut** when detailed financial data isn't available

May Allah accept your zakat and multiply your blessings.
